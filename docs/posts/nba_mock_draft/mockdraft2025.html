2025 NBA Mock Draft

Forecasting the destinations for the next generation of NBA stars
Basketball
Opinion
Author

Danny Nolan

Published

June 25, 2025

The 2025 NBA Draft is upon us, and executives around the league have already kicked into high gear. Impact players like Desmond Bane, Jrue Holiday, and Kristaps Porzingis have already been traded, and the wheeling and dealing is certain to continue throughout the draft, which now spans two days. This year’s draft features a generational prospect, loaded rosters with unusually high selections, and a team with five (5!) first-round choices. Let’s take a look at how the first round might play out.

1. Dallas Mavericks: F Cooper Flagg, Duke

2. San Antonio Spurs: G Dylan Harper, Rutgers

3. Philadelphia 76ers: G VJ Edgecombe, Baylor

4. Charlotte Hornets: F Ace Bailey, Rutgers

5. Utah Jazz: G Tre Johnson, Texas

Flagg and Harper seem like locks to go 1 and 2, but it gets interesting when the 76ers pick. Ace Bailey, probably presumed to go third based on sheer talent, hasn’t worked out for any teams and actually canceled his workout for Philadelphia. His tumultuous pre-draft process clashes with Edgecombe’s, as teams love the Baylor guard’s motor and makeup. The Sixers, who have had their share of first-round flameouts (see Simmons, Ben or Fultz, Markelle), take the safe route and opt for the bouncy Bahamian. Charlotte snaps up Bailey with the fourth pick, as he will have an opportunity to contribute immediately alongside LaMelo Ball and Brandon Miller. Johnson has the potential to develop into an elite scorer. He averaged 19.9 points per game as a freshamn in the SEC and pairs a 6’5’’ frame with a 40% 3-point percentage.

6. Washington Wizards: G Jeremiah Fears, Oklahoma

7. New Orleans Pelicans: C Khaman Maluach, Duke

8. Brooklyn Nets: G Kon Knueppel, Duke

9. Toronto Raptors: G Kasparas Jakucionis, Illinois

10. Phoenix Suns: C Derik Queen, Maryland

With Maluach and Knueppel going 7th and 8th, the lottery is heavy on Blue Devils. Both are safer picks who flourished in their roles at Duke but likely won’t grow into superstars like the top picks could. Fears is on the smaller side (6’2’’), but he is dynamic with the ball in his hands and displayed a knack for clutch buckets at Oklahoma. While they play different positions, Jakucionis and Queen are deft facilitators and would be welcome additions for teams at the back end of the lottery.

11. Portland Trail Blazers: F Noa Essengue, Ratiopharm Ulm

12. Chicago Bulls: F Collin Murray-Boyles, South Carolina

13. Atlanta Hawks: F Carter Bryant, Arizona

14. San Antonio Spurs: G Egor Demin, BYU

15. Oklahoma City Thunder: C Joan Beringer, KK Cedevita Olimpija

Outside of Murray-Boyles, these picks are projects. All are either 18 or 19 years old, and none averaged over 11 points last season. Demin is a supersized 6’8’’ PG, but he only knocked down 27.3% of his threes at BYU. Essengue also struggled from beyond the arc, but he brings size and defense to Portland. Bryant only averaged 6.5 points per game with the Wildcats, though the Hawks may see him as a DeAndre Hunter facsimile. The NBA champs are loaded and have no real weaknesses, so they take a flier on the ultimate project in Beringer. The Frenchman averaged less than 6 PPG in Slovenia. His 7-foot frame comes with remarkable upside.

16. Memphis Grizzlies: F Cedric Coward, Washington St.

17. Minnesota Timberwwolves: F Asa Newell, Georgia

18. Washington Wizards: C Thomas Sorber, Georgetown

19. Brooklyn Nets: C Danny Wolf, Michigan

20. Miami Heat: G Walter Clayton Jr, Florida

You might remember Clayton from the NCAA Tournament, as his unconscious shooting led the Gators to a National Championship. His age is working against him, but Clayton has the tools to help a team win right away. Wolf was another fixture in March who pairs the body of a center with the playmaking ability of a guard. In a season hindered by injury, Sorber averaged 14.5 PPG and 8.5 RPG as a Hoya. He and Fears would be solid building blocks for a Wizards team that has been treading water for too long.

21. Utah Jazz: F Hugo Gonzalez, Real Madrid

22. Brooklyn Nets: G Nolan Traore, Saint-Quentin

23. New Orleans Pelicans: F Liam NcNeeley, Uconn

24. Oklahoma City Thunder: F Noah Penda, Le Mans Sarthe

25. Orlando Magic: F Rasheer Fleming, St. Joseph’s

The Thunder double down on Frenchmen and take Penda at pick 24. It wouldn’t be surprising to see GM Sam Presti move this pick for future assets. Gonzalez and Traore are both 19 years of age and will need time to develop. Utah and Brooklyn are years away from making noise and can give them plenty of playing time. McNeeley, who was teammates with Flagg, Queen, and Newell in high school, can do everything well but lacks a calling card.

26. Brooklyn Nets: F Will Riley, Illinois

27. Brooklyn Nets: F Drake Powell, North Carolina

28. Boston Celtics: G Nique Clifford, Colorado St.

29. Phoenix Suns: C Ryan Kalkbrenner, Creighton

30. Los Angeles Clippers: F Johni Broome, Auburn

If it seems like you’ve been seeing a lot of Nets mentions, it’s because you have. Brooklyn has a staggering five first-round picks, and it would be shocking if none were dealt. The last picks of the round don’t lack for experience. Clifford, Kalkbrenner, and Broome are all 23 and could join an NBA rotation from the get-go. With the injury to Jayson Tatum and trades of Holiday and Porzingis, Boston is in a bridge year and may ask for immediate contributions from Clifford. The Suns are preparing for life without Kevin Durant and currently roster a guard-heavy team. Kalkbrenner provides a serious lob threat and stifling rim protector.
